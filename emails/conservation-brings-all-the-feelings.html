Hi Shannon,

I've spent some time lately in local conservation sites. We joined a group that goes hiking through the woods with our kids every week. This week's hike was led by a certified Texas Naturalist. I didn't grow up here. And the native foliage is so much more drought tolerant, scruffier, and presents in a kaleidoscope of browns.

I grew up in the great state of Ohio. Home to the world famous Cuyahoga River Fires of the 1969s. It made the cover of Time Magazine. THE River Fire that kicked off the environmental conservation movement of the 70s and spread around the world.

My hometown fell so hard for conservation that a restoration plan was completed by 1977. 

And they didn't quit.

They established a national park that has only expanded consistently for decades. 

In 2021 the region is no longer listed as an environmental area of concern.

Just this year they purchased 198 acres to expand their reach. 

The American Beaver populations has returned to the area. Blue Heron populations have become stable and now have an annual breeding ground that is protected. My dad says it's one particular Buckeye tree.🤣

It's listed as the #1 national park in Ohio. And you can see why. 

All of this was accomplished in a community where the average household income dropped below $60,000/year in 2020. It's since increased by 6.7% to $63,000/year.

🌾Visiting the forests here in Dallas County has been an exploration of a very different ecosystem.

🐍We saw a copperhead snake (venomous), wolf spider (venomous), and scorpion fly (non-venomous but looks like it should be). And the grove of protected Texas Buckeyes was eye-opening, all parts of the Texas Buckeye are poisonous.🤦‍♀️ 

My core forest memories are radically different than my children's.

Conservation here in Texas is a different kind of work. Most of the year, hiking through the woods where we ventured into this week means being swarmed with mosquitos so thickly that your clothes turn black.

I have a lot to learn.

This week I also learned something pretty startling. It illuminated my heritage of conservation and the fairytale glow of conservation glories of my childhood in Ohio.

Europe has largely lost their biodiversity. The European Union is putting together their plan for recovery of Europe's biodiversity by 2030. They're still trying to get their ecosystems stable. For them, they're back in Ohio in the 1970s metaphorically speaking.

And there is a lot more to learn about how the damage was accomplished and what they're doing to conserve, recover, and move toward getting off their own lists of environmental concern.

Kyle Lybarger of @nativehabitatproject has been showing me some clever ways to practice conservation everyday. Here are some of his tips.

🌳 Plant the acorns of the oldest trees in your area and pass them out to be planted in your community to preserve the rare DNA of the most radical survivors of our core keystone species. Trees who have been mowed around for decades and been unable to propagate themselves.

🌳 Stop mowing recreationally. This is something where I have a lot of growing to do. And as in his case, it is right to get angry about this.

🌳 Convert your property to a native ecosystem that supplies food and respite to migratory animals and restores the topsoil. Current models predict loss of topsoil by 2060 worldwide.

The story of conservation is so often a call to protest, protect the animals, save the trees. And it does my soul so much good to remember it is always woven together with the lives of real people. Grandparents, parents, kids, cousins, neighbors, who want to make a world that will continue toward health for generations to come. It's the only planet we've got.

And again we hear the hope, perched in our souls, singing through hurricanes, battered by harsh winds, and singing nonetheless. 

With love for the planet,🍂🌼
