Each human is a complex, contradictory story. -Joy Harjo
 
The mosquitos are biting again in Texas. It's the season where we all abandon the woods for a few months of rising and undaunted heat. 
 
A naturalist friend of mine told me that she came too late in the season to a trail head near the Trinity River and within a few minutes her clothes had changed color to a hazy black for all the mosquitos that has found her.
 
When I moved here from Ohio 20 years ago, my culture shock and mosquito shock was powerful. I wanted to eradicate all of them by whatever means necessary. I even found the aggressive Grackles irritating. Big like a crow but they hop around on the ground and attack each other, your friend fries, strutting into traffic and screaming if you cross their path. They're so spectacularly crazy, conservationists have listed their population concern level as zero.
 
Beautiful feathers, terrible personality.
 
My husband says, he loves them. They're the crazy ones.
(Don't read into that too much).
 
Pests. Am I right. 
 
Starting Grove Wisdom was born out of this, I promise. Stay with me.
 
The truth is, I had to move beyond my outsider status in a big way to curb my enthusiasm for eradication.
 
Wasps, fire ants, mosquitos that cover your clothes, scavenger birds that scream at you. There wasn't a lot to love here at the beginning.
 
My time in Texas has been its own kind of odyssey. Facing monsters and unexpected allies.
 
Joy Harjo, The national poet laureate, first Native American to hold the post, of the Muscogee Nation has written this.
 
    Do not feed the monsters.
    Some are wandering thought forms, looking for a place to set up house.
    Some are sent to you deliberately. They come from arrows of gossip, jealousy or envy—and inadvertently from thoughtlessness.
    They feed on your attention, and feast on your fear.
 
 
It has been a long road to leave behind my habit of feeding the monsters. Desiring to eradicate the nuisance. When in reality I offered them my attention and fed my fear.
 
Caring for the earth with these creatures, were part of the whole package is frustrating and intimidating.
 
It's easier to froth with rage and chase them with my lawnmower. (Yes I've done this.)
 
Lack of compassion for annoying little monsters was really a mask for my lack of compassion for myself and anyone else. 
 
I was far from home. The Texan world felt false and foreign in my body. I couldn't feel the weather and know when to grab an umbrella anymore.
 
There was too much sun here and I didn't care for any of it.
 
    The Wanderer
      by Joy Harjo
 
  We are the earth, she told me that day we sat at her kitchen table.
 (Everyone came to her table from the four directions to hear her stories.)
  "One day I will be gone," she said.
  "And what will you remember of what I tell you?"
  I realize now that she was the very Earth herself, talking.
 
 
During the pandemic, we lost our income three times.
 
And I had always wanted to start a business but used the usual reasons. I never went to business school. I don't know how. But the fire had been lit and here we are. A year old and much clearer on why I'm here doing what I'm doing.
 
Your story brought you here.
 
Away from eradication impulses, with a desire to stop feeding the monsters, and a passion to do something to help the Earth.

After all, we belong—the Earth and all those living things.
 
Joy Harjo gave it words from her own story.
 
  For any spark to make a song it must be transformed by pressure. There must be unspeakable need, muscle of belief, and wild, unknowable elements. I am singing a song that can only be born after losing a country.
 
I hope you can put words to your own too.
 
Because it will take all of us to move past a spirit of eradication (even mosquitos—leave those to the bats) and fear to a spirit of belonging and curiosity.
 
  Let's not shame our eyes for seeing. Instead, thank them for their bravery.
-Joy Harjo
 
I'm grateful you're here. Learning not to feed the monsters with me.
